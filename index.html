❓ Why does Government show the lowest unemployment (~2.2%) despite −327K federal job losses?
Three separate effects explain this apparent paradox:
1. Different surveys, different populations. The −327K job loss is from the payroll survey (CES — counts employer jobs). The 2.2% unemployment is from the household survey (CPS — counts people). They don't have to move together.
2. The −327K is cumulative peak-to-trough (Oct 2024 → Mar 2026), not a 2025 annual figure. The 2025 annual net change was −200K. State & local government added +70K in 2025, partially offsetting federal losses. The unemployment sector "Government" covers all government workers — federal, state, and local combined.
3. Displaced federal workers rarely become "unemployed" in BLS terms. To be counted as unemployed you must be actively job-searching. Most affected federal workers: retire early, transfer to state/local roles, move to contractor positions (which are counted in private-sector payrolls), or simply exit the labor force — none of which register as unemployment.

Key 2025 Actuals & 2026 Macro Themes

📊 2025 GDP: resilient but uneven — Full-year +2.2% matched forecasts despite extreme quarterly volatility. Q1 −0.6% was distorted by tariff front-loading (massive import surge). Q2 +3.8% and Q3 +4.4% showed robust consumer and export demand. Q4 slowed to +1.4% due to the 43-day government shutdown — the longest in history. EY noted the year saw "pronounced trade gyrations and a rare constellation of supply shocks."

📉 Jobs revised sharply lower — The Jan 2026 BLS benchmark revision cut 2025 total payrolls by 862,000 — from +1.04M to +181K (≈15K/mo). This is the largest benchmark downward revision since 2009. Healthcare (+630K) and government (+277K) were the only sectors with strong gains. Information tech lost −49K. Federal employment down 327K cumulatively from Oct'24 peak (DOGE) — note this is peak-to-trough across 16+ months, not a single-year total. The 2025 annual net change was −200K. Government unemployment remains low (~2.2%) because displaced federal workers tend to transfer to state/local roles, retire, or were contractors excluded from the payroll survey.

🔄 Tariff drag fading → tax cut tailwind — Goldman Sachs estimates tariffs subtracted ~0.6pp from GDP in H2 2025. With tariff rates largely stable, this mechanical drag fades in 2026. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act delivers ~$100B in additional consumer tax refunds in H1 2026, equivalent to +0.4% of disposable income. Full expensing of capex already boosting business investment indicators.

🤖 AI remains the dominant growth driver — Hyperscaler capex surged +69% in 2025; consensus expects +33% more in 2026. Data center construction and chip spending drove real equipment investment. Goldman and RSM cite AI as "the primary driver of growth for at least another year." Key risk: AI bubble — if ROI disappoints, this $1.5T+ investment wave could reverse sharply.

⚠️ 2026 key risk: stagflation trap — Stanford SIEPR and Morgan Stanley flag stagflation as the main tail risk. Core PCE at 2.8% Dec'25 — above Fed target. If tariffs reignite inflation while unemployment rises (currently 4.3–4.5%), the Fed faces a dilemma: cut rates risks inflation; hold rates risks recession. Three FOMC members dissented on the December 2025 decision — most dissents since 2019. Goldman sees 2 cuts (Jun + Sep); Morgan Stanley sees extended pause at 3.0–3.25%.

🏛️ SCOTUS tariff ruling: the wildcard — Stanford estimates ~75% probability the Supreme Court rules against IEEPA tariff authority. A broad ruling could force rollback of most tariffs. Markets would likely rally sharply on reduced inflation pressure. However, Stanford expects the administration to seek workarounds rather than reverse the trade regime.

📋
Why did Student Loans spike from 0% to 9.4% in Q2 2025? During COVID-19 (Mar 2020 – Sept 2023), federal student loan payments were in administrative forbearance — delinquencies were not reported to credit bureaus. When the SAVE plan lawsuit froze repayments again in 2024, servicers were still required to begin reporting delinquencies to credit bureaus starting Q1–Q2 2025. The jump from 0% → 9.4% is a one-time statistical re-entry of ~5 million accumulated non-payers back into the credit reporting system, not new deterioration. Stability at 9.4% through Q3 2025 confirms the rate is plateauing as the re-entry cohort is fully counted.

📋 Jobs Market Commentary — 2025 & 2026 Outlook
2025 recap: Nonfarm payrolls added a revised +181K/month avg after the Jan 2026 benchmark revision slashed the initial +1.0M estimate by −862K — the largest downward revision since 2009. Gains concentrated in healthcare (+65K/mo avg), government (+42K), and leisure/hospitality (+28K). Manufacturing and retail shed jobs. The Jan 2026 print: +143K, slightly below consensus. Unemployment held at 4.3% — historically low but up from 3.4% trough. JOLTS openings ~7.6M, down from 12M peak; quits rate normalizing to 2019 levels signaling cooling labor confidence. 2026 outlook: Consensus ~130–160K/mo — enough to hold unemployment near 4.3–4.5%. Key risk: federal spending cuts (DOGE) could subtract 50–100K from government payrolls through 2026.
👷 Unemployment Commentary — Trends & Risk Factors
Current picture: U-3 at 4.3% (Jan 2026) — above the 2023 trough of 3.4% but well below historical average of ~5.7%. U-6 broad unemployment (includes discouraged + part-time for economic reasons) at 8.0%, a more complete labor slack picture. Sectoral divergence is widening: Construction & manufacturing unemployment rising (tariff exposure), while healthcare and government remain tight. Duration: Long-term unemployment (27+ weeks) ticking higher — historically a leading indicator of structural labor market stress. 2026 risk scenarios: Base case 4.3–4.5% (GS/JPM); bear case 5.0–5.5% if tariff shock materializes fully; bull case 4.0% if AI productivity unlocks new hiring. The Fed watches U-6 and prime-age employment-population ratio (~80.7%) as cleaner signals than headline U-3.
💵 Wage Growth Commentary — Nominal vs Real
2025 wages: Nominal Average Hourly Earnings grew +3.3% YoY — decelerating from +5.9% peak in 2022 toward pre-pandemic norm of ~3.0%. Real wages (inflation-adjusted) grew +0.7% annualized — positive for the first time since 2020, supporting consumer spending but below the +1.5–2% real gains of the 2010s expansion. Sectoral spread: Tech/finance wages still elevated (+5–7% nominal); retail, leisure, and logistics normalizing. Union contracts (auto, airline, healthcare) locked in 4–5% annual raises through 2027 — creating a wage floor. ECI (Employment Cost Index) at +3.8% Q4 2025 — the Fed's preferred wage gauge, still running above the ~3% compatible with 2% inflation. 2026 outlook: Wage growth converging toward 3.0–3.5% nominal; real wages positive but modest. Risk: services inflation stays sticky if wages stay elevated.
🛒 CPI Commentary — Inflation Trajectory & Fed Implications
2025 CPI: Headline CPI ended 2025 at +2.7% YoY (Dec), down from +9.1% peak in June 2022 — a dramatic disinflation over 30 months. Feb 2026: +2.4%, approaching the Fed's 2% target. Core CPI (ex-food & energy) at +2.5% (Feb'26) — driven by shelter (+3.0%), food (+3.1%), and services inflation. Shelter lag: BLS shelter CPI tracks actual rents with a 12–18 month lag; real-time rent data (Zillow, Apartment List) shows flat-to-negative growth, suggesting shelter CPI will drag toward 2–3% through 2026. Tariff risk: 2026 consensus CPI forecasts range +2.3–3.1%, with the upper bound reflecting full tariff pass-through on consumer goods (apparel, electronics, autos). The Fed is unlikely to cut further if Core CPI stays above 2.5%. Watch: Used cars, OER (owners' equivalent rent), and airfares — the three most volatile core components.
💳 PCE & Consumer Commentary — Spending Health & Household Stress
PCE inflation: Core PCE at +2.6% YoY (Dec 2025) — the Fed's official target gauge. Running above 2% for 48 consecutive months. Real consumer spending grew +2.8% in 2025, supported by strong labor income and residual excess savings — but savings are now largely exhausted for bottom-two income quintiles. Personal saving rate at 4.1% — below the 7–8% pre-pandemic norm, suggesting households are spending more than income growth alone supports. Household debt: $18.0T total (NY Fed Q3 2025); mortgage debt manageable due to locked-in 3% rates; credit card + auto delinquencies rising — card 90+ DPD at 11.3%, auto at 8.9% (subprime most stressed). Consumer bifurcation: Top 40% income households (asset-owners, equity beneficiaries) spending robustly; bottom 40% cutting back on discretionary. Retail sales data reflects this split: luxury and value outperform, mid-market squeezed.
🏠 Housing Commentary — Affordability Crisis & Lock-In Effect
2025 housing: Existing home sales near 30-year lows — the lock-in effect dominates. ~65% of outstanding mortgages carry rates below 4%; with 30yr rates at 6.9%, existing homeowners have zero incentive to sell. New home sales filling the gap — builders offering buy-downs and incentives. Home prices: Case-Shiller up +4.7% YoY nationally (Nov 2025) despite suppressed volume — supply remains near historic lows. NAR Affordability Index at historic low: Median household needs 43% of gross income to afford median home at current rates (norm: ~25–30%). Starts recovery uneven: Single-family starts recovering (+12% in 2025); multi-family collapsing (−28%) as apartment completions flood markets. 2026 outlook: Rates need to fall below 6.0% to meaningfully unlock transaction volume. GS forecasts 30yr at 6.5% year-end 2026. Price appreciation likely slows to +2–3%; no crash absent recession.
